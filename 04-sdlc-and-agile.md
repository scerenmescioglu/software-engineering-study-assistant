# Software Development Life Cycle and Agile Methods

The Software Development Life Cycle, or SDLC, is a structured process used to plan, develop, test, deploy, and maintain software.

## Main SDLC Stages

### Planning

The team identifies:

- The problem to solve
- Project objectives
- Required resources
- Budget
- Schedule
- Risks
- Technical feasibility

### Requirements Analysis

The team collects and analyses the needs of users and other stakeholders.

The output is commonly a Software Requirements Specification.

### System Design

The team decides how the system will be constructed.

Design activities may include:

- Software architecture
- Database design
- User-interface design
- Component design
- Data-flow design
- Security design

### Implementation

Developers translate the system design into source code.

Implementation may also include:

- Code reviews
- Unit testing
- Version control
- Documentation

### Testing

The software is evaluated to identify defects and verify that it meets its requirements.

Testing may include:

- Unit testing
- Integration testing
- System testing
- Acceptance testing

### Deployment

The completed software is released to users or installed in its production environment.

Deployment may be gradual, immediate, or divided into multiple releases.

### Maintenance

After deployment, the system is corrected, improved, and adapted.

Maintenance categories include:

- Corrective maintenance: fixing defects
- Adaptive maintenance: responding to environmental changes
- Perfective maintenance: improving performance or functionality
- Preventive maintenance: reducing future problems

## Waterfall Model

The Waterfall model follows a mostly sequential process.

A stage is normally completed before the next stage begins.

### Advantages

- Simple structure
- Clear documentation
- Predictable milestones
- Suitable for stable requirements
- Easy to manage in highly regulated projects

### Limitations

- Difficult to respond to changing requirements
- Working software appears late
- Testing occurs relatively late
- User feedback may arrive after major decisions are made

## Agile Development

Agile development uses short, iterative development cycles.

Teams deliver small increments of working software and regularly collect stakeholder feedback.

Agile values:

- Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
- Working software over comprehensive documentation
- Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
- Responding to change over following a fixed plan

Agile does not mean that planning, documentation, or processes are unnecessary. It means they should support software delivery rather than prevent adaptation.

## Scrum

Scrum is an Agile framework that organises work into time-boxed iterations called sprints.

A sprint commonly lasts between one and four weeks.

### Scrum Roles

#### Product Owner

The Product Owner:

- Represents stakeholder needs
- Defines product priorities
- Manages the product backlog
- Clarifies requirements
- Accepts completed work

#### Scrum Master

The Scrum Master:

- Helps the team follow Scrum
- Removes obstacles
- Facilitates Scrum events
- Protects the team from unnecessary interruptions
- Encourages continuous improvement

#### Developers

Developers design, implement, test, and deliver the product increment.

## Scrum Artifacts

### Product Backlog

The product backlog is a prioritised list of desired product work.

### Sprint Backlog

The sprint backlog contains the work selected for the current sprint.

### Increment

The increment is the usable product output produced during the sprint.

## Scrum Events

### Sprint Planning

The team selects and plans the work for the sprint.

### Daily Scrum

A short daily meeting used to inspect progress and coordinate work.

### Sprint Review

The team demonstrates the completed increment and gathers feedback.

### Sprint Retrospective

The team discusses what went well, what caused problems, and how to improve the next sprint.

## Kanban

Kanban visualises work using columns such as:

- To Do
- In Progress
- Testing
- Done

Kanban limits work in progress to reduce bottlenecks and improve workflow.

## Agile and Waterfall Comparison

Waterfall is more suitable when requirements are stable, changes are rare, and formal documentation is essential.

Agile is more suitable when requirements may change, frequent feedback is available, and the product can be delivered incrementally.
